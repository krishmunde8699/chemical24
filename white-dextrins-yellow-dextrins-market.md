Dextrins are a group of low-molecular-weight carbohydrates produced by the hydrolysis of starch. Depending on the processing method and conditions, dextrins can be categorized into white dextrins and yellow dextrins. White dextrins are typically produced at lower temperatures and are used in adhesives, coatings, and pharmaceuticals, while yellow dextrins are produced at higher temperatures and are used in foundry, adhesives, and textile industries. End-Use Industries:

Key end-use industries for white and yellow dextrins include:

Adhesives: The adhesives industry is the largest consumer of both white and yellow dextrins. White dextrins are used in paper and packaging adhesives, while yellow dextrins are preferred in the foundry and textile industries for their strong bonding properties.
Coatings: In the coatings industry, white dextrins are used as binders and thickeners in paints and coatings. Their solubility and film-forming properties make them ideal for this application.
Pharmaceuticals: White dextrins are used in the pharmaceutical industry as excipients and carriers for active ingredients. Their biocompatibility and non-toxic nature make them suitable for medicinal formulations.
Textiles: Yellow dextrins are used in the textile industry for sizing and finishing fabrics. Their adhesive properties help improve the texture and durability of textiles.
Food Industry: In the food industry, dextrins are used as thickening agents, stabilizers, and sweeteners in various food products. Their versatility and functional properties make them essential ingredients in food processing.
